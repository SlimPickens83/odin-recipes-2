Bread

May be eaten torn or whole. Improved with fats and salts.

Thy laborers having reaped and ground much barley, thou shalt instruct thy cooks thusly:

  1. Having measured out a stone's worth of grain, collect and place it in a basin.
  2. Setting this aside, pour a stone of iced water, taking a draught for thyself so that not quite a stone's worth remains.
  3. Combine the water and grain and, having added the desirable salt, cycle it thoroughly so that it takes shape.
  4. Take some oil, with it coating both bread pan and lump.
  5. Place thy pan in a fired hovel, not removing hence if it has not dried.
  6. At the appropriate time, remove the fresh bread and set aside to cool.

Pudding

On such occasion as a fresh slaughter, whether beast or enemy, blood can be reserved for a rich pudding which imbues much vigor.

  1. Take together two stones of blood and at least one stone of water.
  2. Add then a heaping pinch of dried honey, also some salt, and less still some dried ginger root.
  3. Do not agitate thy bloody soup lest thou hast also measured and added one stone and half again of ground barley.
  4. Goat butter, or thy desired fat, then add to a fired pan and once active make a thin layer proceeding from thy basin.
  5. Do not delay, for the pudding is quick and will burn unattended.
  6. Thou may also take belly of pork to the fire and crisp it, for this with also a cloudberry preserve is most to be desired.
